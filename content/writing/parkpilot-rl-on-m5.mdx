The premise was simple: train an Ackermann (car-like) robot to reverse or forward into a tight slot between two parked cars, in simulation, on a MacBook M5. No NVIDIA GPU. No Isaac Sim. No CUDA.

This is the story of how that worked, what almost went wrong with the reward function, and what the numbers actually said.

---

## Why not Isaac Sim?

Isaac Sim is where I spend most of my simulation work. It's the obvious choice — but it requires CUDA, and CUDA requires NVIDIA hardware. Apple Silicon has neither. Running Isaac Sim inside a VM or via cloud GPU was an option, but the goal here was a tight, local feedback loop: edit → train → evaluate in one terminal window.

MuJoCo runs natively on Apple Silicon via Metal/CGL for headless rendering. No GPU required for physics or collision. It became the right tool precisely because of the constraint.

---

## Stack

- `uv` + Python 3.12
- MuJoCo 3.9 + Gymnasium 1.0
- Stable-Baselines3 SAC
- PyTorch (CPU)

The entire training run happens on CPU. Phase 3 (fixed start, 100k steps) completes in ~292 seconds.

---

## The kinematic bicycle model trick

MuJoCo gives you scene management, rendering, and collision. It does not give you an Ackermann drive model out of the box. The usual route is `mj_step` — MuJoCo's physics integrator — but Ackermann kinematics are not naturally expressed as MuJoCo forces or joints in a simple way.

The solution: write the motion as a **pure-Python bicycle model** and apply it directly to `qpos`, then call `mj_forward` (not `mj_step`) to update the scene state.

The equations:

```
x  += v · cos(θ) · dt
y  += v · sin(θ) · dt
θ  += (v / L) · tan(δ) · dt
```

Where `v` is drive velocity, `θ` is heading, `L` is wheelbase, and `δ` is steering angle. Each timestep, the Python env computes the new pose, writes it to `model.qpos`, and calls `mj_forward`. MuJoCo provides the physics world and collision checking; the bicycle model provides motion.

This is a deliberate split of responsibilities — and it meant I could swap in domain-randomized wheelbases (Phase 4) without touching the MuJoCo XML at all.

## Environment design

**Observation space (10-D):**

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| x, y | Robot position |
| sin θ, cos θ | Heading (avoids angle wrap) |
| v, δ | Current drive velocity and steer angle |
| goal_dx, goal_dy | Displacement to goal slot |
| sin(Δθ), cos(Δθ) | Heading error to goal |

**Action space (2-D, continuous):**

- Drive: `[-2, 2]` m/s
- Steer: `[-0.6, 0.6]` rad

**Reward:**

Dense shaping reward combining distance-to-goal, heading alignment, and a small time penalty per step. Terminal rewards: +200 for success, −100 for collision or out-of-bounds exit.

Success condition: within **0.15 m** of the slot center and within **±10°** of target heading.

---

## The reward bug that almost wasn't

During design, I flagged what I called the **"suicide-exploit"**: an agent might learn that ending the episode early via collision (−100 once) is better than continuing to accumulate dense penalty from dawdling. A typical unsuccessful episode accumulates roughly −700 to −1100 in dense penalties over its lifetime. A single terminal −100 from immediately fleeing out-of-bounds is cheaper.

This is a real failure mode in sparse/dense hybrid reward design, and I considered rebalancing the terminal penalties upward.

I didn't — and it turned out not to matter. Across **2,835 evaluation episodes**:

| Outcome | Count |
|---------|-------|
| Success | 2652 |
| Collision | 57 |
| Out-of-bounds | 20 |
| Timeout | 106 |

The reward was balanced as-is. The agent converged to parking, not fleeing — and the out-of-bounds "escapes" it was supposed to be tempted by stayed in the noise (20 of 2,835). No tuning needed.

## Training results

**Phase 3 — fixed start position:**

- Steps: 100k
- Wall time: ~292s on CPU
- Episode reward: −696 → +184
- Success rate: **100%**, parks in ~17 steps

![deterministic policy parks the car, fixed start](/parkpilot/parkpilot-demo.gif)

**Phase 4 — domain randomization:**

Randomized: start pose, wheelbase (0.18–0.28 m), observation noise. 300k steps (~15 min CPU). Evaluated across 4 seeds (400 episodes total).

- Randomized-start success: **99.5%** (398/400)
- Fixed-start success: still **100%**

![episode reward + success rate over 300k steps](/parkpilot/parkpilot-curves.png)

The success rate curve from Phase 3 to Phase 4 tells the real story: the domain randomization drop is small and recovers quickly, because the kinematic model is correct and the observation encoding is rotation-invariant.

---

## What I'd change

The bicycle model's wheelbase randomization in Phase 4 is injected at the Python level. A cleaner approach would be a MuJoCo XML template with parameterized geometry, so the rendered scene actually reflects the sampled wheelbase. Currently, the scene always shows the default geometry while the kinematics use the sampled `L`. Functionally fine for training; problematic if you want the simulation to be a visual truth.
